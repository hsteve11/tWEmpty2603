To determine if the 17-year-old you're employing is a trainee, you need to look for a **formal, registered training contract**—it's a specific legal status, not just learning on the job.

Here is the step-by-step process to check:

### ✅ Step 1: Look for the "Training Contract"

The clearest sign of a genuine trainee is a **signed training contract**. This is a legally binding agreement between the employer and the employee. It must be registered with the relevant state or territory training authority.

This contract proves that the employee is formally working towards a recognized qualification (like a Certificate II in Agriculture) through a **Registered Training Organisation (RTO)**. Most traineeships last between 1 and 4 years, depending on whether they are full-time or part-time.

> **Important Note:** A "trainee" is generally for a non-trade qualification (e.g., agriculture, business). If the training is for a trade (like becoming a mechanic or butcher), this would usually be called an **apprenticeship** instead.

### 🧑‍🌾 Step 2: Compare Against a "Farm Hand" Classification

If the employee does **not** have a formal training contract, they are likely just a **Junior Farm Hand**, not a trainee.

According to the **Pastoral Award 2020** (which covers beef and dairy farming), a standard farm hand has clear skill levels:

- **Level 1 (FLH1):** A new starter with under 6 months experience doing basic farm chores (e.g., helping with milking, simple fence repairs, yard work) under close supervision.
- **Level 2 (FLH2):** Has some experience and can handle a tractor or forklift, but still requires regular supervision.

If these tasks describe the 17-year-old's work, and there is no training contract, **he is a standard junior employee, not a trainee**.

### 💰 Step 3: Understand Why This Matters (Pay & Rules)

The distinction matters because the pay structure is different:

| Feature | Standard Junior Farm Hand | Trainee |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Governing Document** | Pastoral Award 2020 | Miscellaneous Award / National Training Wage |
| **Pay Basis** | Percentage of the adult rate for that classification level (FLH1, etc.) | National Training Wage (specific trainee rates) |
| **Formal Study** | No | Yes (must attend TAFE or RTO) |

A trainee wage might be different (sometimes lower or structured as a weekly training wage rather than an hourly rate), but regardless, **it requires a formal contract**.

### ⚠️ Step 4: Watch Out for "Introductory" Periods

There is a nuance to be aware of. The Pastoral Award recently changed to remove "sub-minimum" introductory rates. From **1 April 2025**, an entry-level employee must be paid at least the **National Minimum Wage** ($24.95/hr for adults).

*   If you are currently paying the 17-year-old a very low "introductory" rate because he is "learning," that classification might no longer be valid under the new rules. This is different from being a "Trainee." This is simply an entry-level worker.

### 📝 Summary Checklist

To determine his status, ask these three questions:

1.  **Is there a signed Training Contract** registered with the state? (Yes = Trainee; No = Junior Farm Hand)
2.  **Is he enrolled in a formal qualification** at a TAFE or RTO while working? (Yes = Trainee; No = Junior Farm Hand)
3.  **Am I providing paid study time** (as per the contract)? (Yes = Trainee; No = Junior Farm Hand)

If the answer to these is "No" or "I'm just teaching him how to drive the tractor," then **he is a standard Junior employee** covered by the standard pay rates and public holiday rules mentioned previously.

You can always use the **Fair Work Ombudsman's "Find my award" tool** or call them directly to get a definitive answer based on the exact duties involved.
